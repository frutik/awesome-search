# Complex Search-Results Pages Change Search Behavior: The Pinball Pattern

**Source:** https://www.nngroup.com/articles/pinball-pattern-search-behavior/ — [[Nielsen Norman Group]]

Modern, feature-rich results pages have changed how people scan results: from an orderly top-to-bottom sweep to a **nonlinear "pinball" path** that ricochets between organic results and SERP features (snippets, carousels, knowledge panels).

## The Shift

| | 2006–2010 (linear) | 2017–2019 (pinball) |
|---|---|---|
| Scanning | Sequential; 59% examined results in order | Nonlinear, attention bounces |
| Top-result clicks | 51% | 28% |
| Lower positions | Rarely examined | Position 6 looked at in 36% of cases |

SERP features, when present, drew looks in **74%** of cases — visual weight, not rank alone, now steers attention. Users spend ~5.7s before the first click; research tasks explore below the fold more (20%) than fact-finding tasks (5%).

## Why It Matters

- The classic [[Position Bias]] story (rank 1 dominates) weakens on complex layouts — salience competes with position.
- For **site search**, consistent, simple layouts reduce cognitive load and outperform inconsistent designs.
- First-page presence is still critical: only ~2% of users go beyond page one.

## Method

NN/g Search Meta-Analysis Project — 471 user queries across 2017–2019 eyetracking/usability studies.
