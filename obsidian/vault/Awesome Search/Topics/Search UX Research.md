# Search UX Research

Independent research organizations that study **how people actually use search interfaces** — and turn those observations into evidence-based design guidelines. They sit apart from search vendors and platform marketing: their output is empirical (usability testing, eye-tracking, log analysis, large-scale benchmarking) rather than feature-driven, which is why their findings anchor so much of [[Search UX]].

This note is the directory of those organizations and what each is known for.

---

## Why a Separate Category

Most search writing comes from *engineers* (how to build retrieval/ranking) or *vendors* (why our product is better). UX research orgs fill a different gap: **what real users do in front of a search box and a results page**, measured rather than assumed. Their guidelines are how practitioners justify UX decisions — facet ordering, no-results layouts, autocomplete patterns, scope selectors — without re-running studies themselves.

## The Organizations

| Organization | Focus | Known for |
|---|---|---|
| [[Baymard Institute]] | E-commerce usability | Quantified guidelines ("N% get it wrong"); query-type taxonomy; autocomplete, facets, search scopes, no-results |
| [[Nielsen Norman Group]] | UX research & consulting (web-wide) | Results-page scanning (pinball, love-at-first-sight); scoped search; search-log analysis; good abandonment |
| [[Enterprise Knowledge LLC]] | Enterprise findability & taxonomy | Human-centered search design for enterprise/knowledge management |
| [[Heather Hedden|The Accidental Taxonomist]] | Taxonomy & facet design | How many facets a taxonomy should have; when hierarchy helps or hurts |
